Corruption (CPI)

This page focuses on the Corruption Perceptions Index (CPI) and how perceived corruption relates to the distribution of billionaire wealth across countries.

Viz C1 · CPI vs Total Billionaire Wealth

Scatter — perceived corruption vs country billionaire wealth totals

Insights

This chart compares CPI scores with total billionaire wealth by country, suggesting that higher CPI (lower perceived corruption) often coincides with larger pools of billionaire wealth because stable rules and predictable investment climates help large fortunes scale over time. At the same time, a few high-wealth outliers at lower CPI scores show that extreme wealth can still accumulate in weaker institutional contexts, especially when specific sectors dominate or market power is highly concentrated. Overall, CPI is not a single “cause” of billionaire wealth, but it is a meaningful contextual factor that helps explain why some countries are better at sustaining large, compounding wealth totals than others.

Viz C2 · CPI vs Billionaire Count

Scatter — perceived corruption vs number of billionaires

Insights

Countries with higher CPI scores frequently host more billionaires, consistent with the idea that stronger institutions reduce friction for entrepreneurship, scaling businesses, and attracting investment. However, the relationship is not perfectly linear: some countries with moderate CPI still show meaningful billionaire presence, implying that market size, industry mix, and policy also shape billionaire concentration. The overall pattern supports a balanced conclusion: governance quality appears to be an important enabling condition for building a broader billionaire ecosystem, even if it does not fully determine where every billionaire is located.

Note: CPI is perception-based and should be interpreted as a comparative indicator rather than a direct measure of actual corruption.
